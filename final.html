What time is it on the clock of the world?



The Western canon serves a very primary function in shaping thought, one that is seldom overtly recognized. It is a record of the thoughts and ideas and imaginations of the times it was written.



To this, I pose the question, how can a Western logic of time truly encompass a dynamic view of history and historioraphy that falls outside its own bounds?



Inspired by the Smithsonian Open Access database and by the celebrated post-colonial academic Gayatri Spivak's claim that "that crises around life can be analyzed as a result of unequal time logics", I set out to repatriate -- even if for a moment -- a way of keeping time that is flattened out of the dominant chronology and a way of record keeping.

Artifacts by Empire and Dynasty


This project aims to put the vast records of the Arts of the Islamic world back in the way time was kept back then; by Caliphates, Dynasties, and Empires. As is evident, many of these timelines overlap, many exist at the same time or incongruently, and many resist a linear temporal evaluation when so many different regions and countries affect how "time" is seen.


The following graph helps reimagine the "flow" of contribution of each Empire in fashioning the material world of Islamic history & culture, recorded by the dynasty or empire these relics of craftsmanship originated from.

Where were these empires?

These majestic empires today form the nation states of the Middle East. With a rich, artistic, and meticulous-to-detail artisanal culture, I can't help but think how the treatment of the countries in this region is prohibiting any sort of archives and artifact repositories from forming for future generations to look back on. May the resources we have disposable to us help us not only safeguard the future, but may the humanity we have today also help us document the present with oral histories.
